# Cargo Cult Programming

**In brief:** Imitating the visible form of a practice, method, or successful organization while missing the content that makes it work, so the ritual is performed but none of the benefit arrives.

**Category:** Anti-pattern
**Source:** Chapter 9, Topic 50 "Coconuts Don't Cut It"
**Also known as:** cargo cult, coconut airport

## What it is
Anthropologists coined "cargo cult" for the Melanesian islanders who, after wartime planes stopped coming, rebuilt a facsimile airport, control tower, and equipment out of vines, coconut shells, and palm fronds. Everything looked right, but the planes never came because they had imitated the form and not the content. The book applies the label to software teams that invest in easily visible artifacts and hope the underlying working magic will follow.

The concrete symptom is a team that adopts the vocabulary and tooling of a method without its substance: a "daily" standup held weekly, "iterations" that stretch far past their nominal length, ceremonies performed as if the words themselves were incantations for the superstitious. The team invests in superficial structures, often in name only, and then wonders why the real benefit never appears.

The same failure happens one level up when an organization copies the policies and processes of Spotify, Netflix, Stripe, or GitLab without matching their context. Reproducing another company's artifacts is coconut airport thinking: the shells are in place but the planes still do not land.

## Why it matters
Cargo cult programming is expensive because it looks like progress. The team spends real effort on rituals and tools and reports that it is "doing agile," yet the actual capability those practices were meant to produce, working software delivered when users need it, never materializes. Because the artifacts are visible and the missing benefit is not, the gap can persist for a long time before anyone notices the planes are not coming.

## In practice
Ask why you are using a given method, framework, or technique, and whether it is genuinely suited to the job at hand or was adopted because it worked for someone else's success story. Judge practices by whether they help you deliver, not by whether they match a fashionable name. Pilot ideas, keep what works, and discard the ritual. Copy values and outcomes, not coconut-shell artifacts.
