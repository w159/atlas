# Pride and Prejudice

**In brief:** Sign your work and take pride and ownership in it, so your name becomes a mark of quality, while treating others' code with the mutual respect that keeps ownership from turning into territorial prejudice.

**Category:** Mindset
**Source:** Chapter 9, Topic 53 "Pride and Prejudice"
**Also known as:** sign your work, pride of ownership

## What it is
The final topic of the book is a direct consequence of all the rest. Pragmatic Programmers do not shirk responsibility; they rejoice in accepting challenges and in making their expertise known. If you are responsible for a design or a piece of code, you do a job you can be proud of. Artisans of an earlier age were proud to sign their work, and you should be too.

The prejudice half is the danger that comes with ownership. On some projects the idea of code ownership causes cooperation problems: people become territorial, unwilling to work on common foundations, and the project turns into a bunch of insular little fiefdoms where you grow prejudiced in favor of your own code and against your coworkers. That is not the goal. You should not jealously defend your code against interlopers, and you should treat other people's code with respect. The Golden Rule and a foundation of mutual respect among developers are critical to making the "sign your work" tip actually work.

Ownership does not have to mean individual ownership. Anonymity, especially on large projects, breeds sloppiness, mistakes, sloth, and bad code, because it is too easy to see yourself as just a cog producing lame excuses in status reports instead of good code. Code must be owned, but Kent Beck's eXtreme Programming shows it can be owned communally, provided you add practices like pair programming to guard against the dangers of anonymity.

What the authors want to see is pride of ownership: "I wrote this, and I stand behind my work." Your signature should come to be recognized as an indicator of quality, so that people see your name on a piece of code and expect it to be solid, well written, tested, and documented. A really professional job. Written by a professional. A Pragmatic Programmer.

## Why it matters
Signing your work ties your reputation to its quality, which raises the bar on everything you produce and makes your name a trusted signal. Anonymity does the opposite, giving cover for sloppiness and sloth. But pride taken too far curdles into prejudice: territorial ownership blocks collaboration on shared foundations and fractures a team into fiefdoms. Balancing pride with the Golden Rule and mutual respect keeps individual accountability without sacrificing the cooperation the team needs, and communal ownership plus practices like pair programming can capture both.

## In practice
Take responsibility for your designs and code and put your name to them, aiming for work solid enough that your signature signals quality. Do not hide in anonymity or reduce yourself to a cog writing excuses. At the same time, do not hoard or defend your code against teammates, and treat their code with the respect you want for your own. Where the team uses communal ownership, add supporting practices such as pair programming to guard against the sloppiness anonymity invites.
