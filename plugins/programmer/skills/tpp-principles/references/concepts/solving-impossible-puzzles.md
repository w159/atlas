# Solving Impossible Puzzles

**In brief:** When a problem looks impossible, it usually is not; the trick is to identify the real constraints and the degrees of freedom you actually have, and to get out of your own way while your subconscious works.

**Category:** Mindset
**Source:** Chapter 8, Topic 46 "Solving Impossible Puzzles"
**Also known as:** Get Out of Your Own Way, Fortune Favors the Prepared Mind

## What it is
Every so often a project throws up a really tough puzzle: a piece of engineering you cannot get a handle on, or code far harder to write than you expected. Like the little wood-and-iron puzzles that turn up at garage sales, the obvious solutions do not work, yet people keep trying the same thing over and over, thinking there must be a way.

The secret is to identify the real, not imagined, constraints and find a solution within them. Some constraints are absolute and must be honored however distasteful they seem. Others are merely preconceived notions. As Alexander did with the Gordian Knot, some apparent constraints are not real constraints at all.

When a problem feels much harder than it should be, that is the ideal time to do something else. Walk the dog, sleep on it, let your subconscious associative brain work. Distracted people outperform people grinding at conscious effort on complex problems. If you cannot drop it, explain the problem to someone else; the act of talking it through (Rubber Ducking) often leads to enlightenment.

To have eureka moments, your nonconscious brain needs raw material: prior experience it can draw on. Fortune favors the prepared mind. Feeding your brain feedback on what works and what does not (for example, via an Engineering Daybook) builds that raw material.

## Why it matters
Treating imagined constraints as real is what makes problems look impossible. Recognizing them frees the solution that was there all along. Grinding harder at a stuck problem wastes schedule and morale; deliberately stepping back is often faster than pushing through. And a mind stocked with prior experience produces the insights that solve the genuinely hard cases.

## In practice
- Enumerate every possible avenue, no matter how stupid it sounds, then go through the list and prove why each cannot be taken. Are you sure? Can you prove it?
- Categorize and prioritize constraints: like a woodworker cutting the longest pieces first, identify the most restrictive constraints first and fit the rest within them.
- When stuck, change activity: walk, sleep, or work on something else and let the answer surface.
- Explain the problem to someone; have them ask why you are solving it, what the benefit is, whether the trouble is edge cases you can eliminate, and whether there is a simpler related problem.
- Keep feeding your brain with real feedback so it has material for later insights.
- DON'T PANIC.
