# Communicate

**In brief:** Treat your native language as another programming language: the best idea is an orphan unless you can convey it, so know your audience and package what you say as carefully as what you build.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 1, Topic 7 "Communicate"
**Also known as:** English is Just Another Programming Language

## What it is
Having the best ideas, the finest code, or the most pragmatic thinking is ultimately sterile unless you can communicate it to other people. A good idea is an orphan without effective communication. Developers communicate on many levels: meetings, end users, code that documents intent, proposals and memos, and daily advocacy within teams. A large part of the day is spent communicating, so it is worth doing well.

The core move is to treat English, or whatever your native tongue is, as just another programming language. Write natural language the way you write code: honor DRY, ETC, and automation.

The topic then lays out a practical checklist for effective communication, closing with documentation as a form of communication in its own right.

## Why it matters
Unless you work in a vacuum, you need to communicate, and the more effective that communication, the more influential you become. It is both what you say and the way you say it. Poorly packaged ideas fail regardless of their merit, the way a chef can slave for hours and ruin the meal with poor presentation. Communication is a transaction: the meaning of your communication is the response you get, so the burden of being understood is on you.

## In practice
The book's summary checklist:
- Know what you want to say. Plan it and write an outline before you start typing; refine it until it says what you mean to your audience.
- Know your audience. Understand their needs, interests, and capabilities, and pitch the same change differently to end users, marketing, managers, and developers. Gather feedback; ask for questions, watch body language.
- Choose your moment. Work out your audience's priorities and make your message relevant in time as well as content. Sometimes just ask, "Is this a good time to talk about...?"
- Choose a style. Match formality and depth to the audience, from a "just the facts" briefing to a quick tl;dr. If in doubt, ask. Remember you are half the transaction; push back if a request does not fit.
- Make it look good. Presentation matters. Use style sheets, headers and footers, and spell-check by machine and by hand.
- Involve your audience. Share early drafts, get feedback, pick their brains. The process often matters more than the document, and you build a working relationship.
- Be a listener. If you want people to listen to you, listen to them. Ask questions, turn the meeting into a dialog.
- Get back to people. Always respond to emails and voicemails, even if only "I'll get back to you later." Keeping people informed makes them more forgiving.

Online communication: everything above applies equally to email, social media posts, and blogs. Proofread before you hit send, check spelling and auto-correct mishaps, keep the format simple, keep quoting to a minimum, attribute and inline-quote other people's words, don't flame or troll, and check the recipient list before sending. Email and social media posts are forever, so give them the same care as any written memo or report.

Documentation: Pragmatic Programmers build documentation in rather than bolting it on, and apply all their pragmatic principles to it. Generate good-looking docs from source comments, and comment modules and exported functions. But do not mechanically comment every function, structure, and declaration; that creates two things to update and violates DRY. Restrict non-API comments to why something is done, its purpose and goal, and use them to capture engineering trade-offs, discarded alternatives, and the reasons decisions were made.
