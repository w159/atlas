# Mob Programming

**In brief:** An extension of pair programming where more than two people, including non-developers, work the same problem together with one typist.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 8, Topic 47 "Working Together"
**Also known as:** none

## What it is
If two heads are better than one, mob programming asks what happens with a dozen diverse people working the same problem at once with a single typist. Despite the name, it involves no torches or pitchforks. It is an extension of pair programming to more than two developers, and you can think of it as tight collaboration with live coding.

Mobs can easily include people not usually considered part of the development team: users, project sponsors, and testers. In the authors' first "impossible" project, it was common for one of them to be typing while the other discussed the issue with the business expert, a small mob of three. Proponents report great results using mobs to solve hard problems and brainstorm new ideas.

## Why it matters
A mob brings many diverse perspectives to a single hard problem at the same time, which is powerful for thorny issues and brainstorming. Including users, sponsors, and testers directly in the coding session means the software reflects that broad involvement rather than being filtered through documents and meetings.

## In practice
- Use a mob to brainstorm new ideas or diagnose thorny issues.
- Start small: mob with only 4 to 5 people, in short sessions.
- Swap out the typist every 5 to 10 minutes.
- Include your extended team where useful: users, testers, and sponsors, not just developers.
- Follow the rules and guidelines for the style; do some reading before jumping into your toughest production code.
