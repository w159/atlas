# Your Knowledge Portfolio

**In brief:** Manage everything you know as if it were a financial portfolio: invest regularly, diversify, balance risk, buy low, and review and rebalance.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 1, Topic 6 "Your Knowledge Portfolio"
**Also known as:** Knowledge Portfolio, Invest Regularly in Your Knowledge Portfolio

## What it is
Your knowledge and experience are your most important day-to-day professional assets. Unfortunately they are expiring assets: knowledge goes out of date as new techniques, languages, and environments appear, and shifting markets can render experience obsolete quickly. As the value of your knowledge declines, so does your value to your employer or client. Your ability to learn new things is your most important strategic asset.

The authors think of everything a programmer knows, about computing, about their application domains, and about their experience, as a knowledge portfolio, and managing it works much like managing a financial one. Serious investors invest regularly as a habit. Diversification is the key to long-term success. Smart investors balance conservative and high-risk, high-reward holdings. They try to buy low and sell high. And they review and rebalance periodically.

Managing this investment is a learnable skill. The trick is to make yourself do it until it becomes a habit, so that eventually you soak up new knowledge automatically.

## Why it matters
Left alone, a knowledge portfolio depreciates, and a developer whose skills stagnate becomes less valuable over time. The most important of all the guidelines is also the simplest: invest regularly. Even small, consistent contributions compound, and the process of learning itself expands your thinking and opens new ways of doing things. The cross-pollination matters: learn object orientation and you write procedural code differently; understand functional programming and you write object-oriented code differently.

## In practice
Apply the five investment guidelines:
- Invest regularly, even a small amount, at a consistent time and place away from interruptions. The habit matters as much as the sums.
- Diversify. The more different things you know, the more valuable and adaptable you are. Include non-technical skills.
- Manage risk. Spread investments across risky high-reward and low-risk low-reward technologies. Do not put all your technical eggs in one basket.
- Buy low, sell high. Learning an emerging technology before it is popular is hard but can pay off handsomely, as early Java adopters found.
- Review and rebalance. Last month's hot technology may be cold now; brush up or reposition as the industry shifts.

Concrete goals the book suggests:
- Learn at least one new language every year.
- Read a technical book each month, and nontechnical books too (the human side matters).
- Take classes; participate actively in local user groups and meetups.
- Experiment with different environments; stay current by reading about technology outside your current project.

It does not matter whether you ever ship these technologies or list them on your resume. The learning itself is the return.

Opportunities for learning: when someone asks a question you cannot answer, do not let it stop there. Take it as a personal challenge to find the answer, ask around, and search the web (the scholarly parts too). If you cannot find the answer, find out who can; talking to people builds your network. Plan ahead so you always have something to read in an otherwise dead moment.

Critical thinking: think critically about what you read and hear, so the knowledge in your portfolio stays accurate and unswayed by vendor or media hype. Never underestimate the power of commercialism: a top search hit or a prominently displayed book may simply be paid placement. Beware of zealots whose dogma may not apply to you or your project. Questions the book suggests:
- Ask the "Five Whys": ask "why?" at least five times to dig toward a root cause.
- Who does this benefit? Follow the money.
- What's the context? A "best practice" is best for someone; ask for whom, and what the prerequisites and short- and long-term consequences are.
- When or where would this work? Use second-order thinking: not just what happens next, but what happens after that.
- Why is this a problem? Is there an underlying model, and how does it work?
