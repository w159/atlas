# Pragmatic Teams

**In brief:** Apply the pragmatic practices of the individual programmer at the level of the whole team, so a small, stable group operates as one entity that owns its quality, awareness, communication, and delivery.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 9, Topic 49 "Pragmatic Teams"
**Also known as:** none

## What it is
The book spends most of the book teaching techniques for the individual programmer, then asks whether those same techniques scale to teams of strong-willed, independent people. The answer is yes, and the advantages of being pragmatic multiply when the individual works on a pragmatic team. A team here is a small, mostly stable entity of its own, under 10 to 12 members, where people come and go rarely and everyone knows, trusts, and depends on each other. Fifty people are a horde, not a team, and a rotating cast of strangers is not a team either.

The topic recasts earlier individual practices in team terms. No Broken Windows becomes a shared duty: quality is a team issue that cannot be delegated to a "quality officer," because quality comes only from the individual contributions of all members. Quality is built in, not bolted on. Boiled Frogs warns that teams get cooked even more easily than individuals, because everyone assumes someone else is watching the environment, so the whole team must actively monitor for scope creep and changing requirements.

Other individual ideas get the same treatment. Schedule your Knowledge Portfolio: if a team wants to improve, it must schedule time for maintenance, process reflection, tech experiments, and learning, because "whenever there is a free moment" never arrives. Communicate: the team as an entity has a presence in the organization and must speak with one voice, which the book suggests reinforcing by branding the project with a name and a logo. Don't Repeat Yourselves: avoid stovepipe or siloed duplication through instant, frictionless communication, so members stay aware of what others are doing.

Two structural ideas round it out. Team Tracer Bullets: build fully functional teams that hold all the skills (frontend, UI/UX, server, DBA, QA) needed to deliver a feature end to end, rather than splitting into specialized teams that create gates, handoffs, and waste. Automation: automate everything the team does for consistency and accuracy. And Know When to Stop Adding Paint: give each member room to shine, provide just enough structure, then resist adding more.

## Why it matters
A pragmatic team turns individual good habits into a compounding advantage. Without shared ownership, the most diligent developer loses the will to fix niggling problems on a team that does not care, and broken windows spread. Without shared vigilance, a well-intentioned team drifts into scope creep and gets boiled. Siloed teams duplicate work and create maintenance nightmares. Teams split by role introduce artificial gates, handoffs, and approvals that stop the smooth flow of work, and programmers removed from real users cannot make informed decisions. A functional, communicating team ships incrementally and can tune itself quickly.

## In practice
Keep teams small and stable so trust and dependence can form. Make quality everyone's job, never a delegated role. Have everyone watch the environment for scope, schedule, and requirement changes, and keep metrics (a burnup chart shows moving goalposts better than a burndown chart). Put maintenance, process refinement, tech experiments, and learning on the schedule, not in the leftover time. Give the project a memorable brand and speak with one voice externally while encouraging lively debate internally. Communicate instantly and with low ceremony, in person or by messaging app. Build fully functional teams that can drive a small feature end to end with a tracer bullet, then use that feedback to tune the team. Automate formatting, testing, and deployment. Give members structure enough to deliver, then stop adding paint.
