# Delight Your Users

**In brief:** Aim to delight users by understanding and delivering the business value they actually expect, not merely by shipping working code against a specification.

**Category:** Mindset
**Source:** Chapter 9, Topic 52 "Delight Your Users"
**Also known as:** delight users don't just deliver code, be a problem solver

## What it is
Our goal as developers is to delight users. Not to mine their data, count their eyeballs, or empty their wallets, and even delivering working software on time is not enough by itself to delight them. Users are not particularly motivated by code. They have a business problem to solve within their objectives and budget, and they believe that working with your team will let them solve it. Their expectations are not software related and are not even implicit in any specification they give you, because that specification stays incomplete until the team has iterated through it with them several times.

To unearth those expectations, ask a simple question: how will you know that we have all been successful a month, or a year, after this project is done? The answer can be surprising. A project to improve product recommendations might actually be judged by customer retention; a project to consolidate two databases might be judged by data quality or cost savings. What counts is the expectation of business value, not the software project itself. The software is only a means to those ends.

Once you have surfaced the underlying value expectations, you can deliver against them: make sure everyone on the team is clear about them, weigh decisions by which path moves closer to them, critically analyze the stated requirements in their light, and keep thinking about them as the project progresses. The book warns that a stated "requirement" is often just a guess at what technology could do, an amateur implementation plan dressed up as a requirements document, so do not be afraid to suggest changes that move the project closer to the real objective.

As your knowledge of the domain grows, you become better able to suggest other things that address the underlying business issues. Developers, exposed to many parts of an organization, can often see ways to weave different parts of the business together that individual departments miss. If you want to delight a client, forge a relationship where you actively help solve their problems. Your title might say Software Developer or Software Engineer, but in truth it should be Problem Solver. That is what a Pragmatic Programmer does: we solve problems.

## Why it matters
Success is in the eye of the beholder, the sponsor of the project, and their perception is what counts. A bug-free system that answers the wrong question is not very useful, so meeting a specification while missing the business value fails the only test that matters. Surfacing value expectations early aligns the whole team, guides day-to-day decisions, and lets you challenge requirements that are really disguised implementation guesses. Treating yourself as a problem solver, not a code producer, builds the relationship and domain knowledge that let you deliver outcomes the user did not even know to ask for.

## In practice
Ask users how they will judge success a month or a year out, and listen for the business measure behind the software request. Make those value expectations explicit to the whole team and use them to weigh every decision. Analyze stated requirements critically and propose changes when they move the project closer to the real objective. Keep revisiting the expectations as your domain knowledge grows, and look for cross-department opportunities others miss. Act as a problem solver who forges a helping relationship with the client, not just a deliverer of code.
