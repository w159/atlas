# Listen to Your Lizard Brain

**In brief:** Pay attention to instincts and nagging doubts while coding, because your nonconscious brain is feeding back pattern knowledge that has no words.

**Category:** Mindset
**Source:** Chapter 7, Topic 37 "Listen to Your Lizard Brain"
**Also known as:** Listen to Your Inner Lizard, listen to your gut, trust your instincts

## What it is
Instincts are a response to patterns packed into your nonconscious brain. Some are innate, others learned through repetition. As you gain experience, your brain lays down layers of tacit knowledge: what works, what does not, the probable cause of a type of error. This is the part of your brain that hits save when you stop to chat, before you realize you did it.

Instincts have no words. They make you feel, not think. When one is triggered you do not see a flashing lightbulb, you just get nervous, or queasy, or feel like something is too much work. Two common signals: fear of the blank page (reluctance to start), and "fighting yourself," where coding feels like walking uphill in mud because the code is telling you the structure, design, or problem is wrong.

The trick is to first notice it is happening, then work out why. The doubt may be experience trying to speak to you, or it may be a reasonable fear of making a mistake, sometimes tinged with imposter syndrome.

## Why it matters
Ignoring these signals means soldiering on with a bad design, solving the wrong problem, or generating an ant farm's worth of bugs. Heeding them lets your accumulated experience contribute to your performance before the problem jumps out at you. The same instinct applies beyond code: a design or requirement that "feels wrong" deserves the same stop-and-analyze treatment.

## In practice
When you feel the signal, the technique is always the same. First, stop what you are doing. Give your brain time and space by doing something mindless away from the keyboard: take a walk, have lunch, chat, sleep on it. Let the ideas percolate up on their own.

If that does not work, externalize the issue. Doodle about the code, explain it to a coworker (preferably not a programmer) or to your rubber duck.

If you are still stuck, take action by prototyping. Tell your brain what you are about to do does not matter:

1. Write "I'm prototyping" on a sticky note and stick it on your screen.
2. Remind yourself that prototypes are meant to fail and get thrown away, so there is no downside.
3. In your empty editor, write a one-sentence comment describing what you want to learn or do.
4. Start coding.

If doubts return, look at the sticky note. When a nagging doubt crystallizes into a solid concern, address it. When the nervousness evaporates and you feel urgency to get it done, delete all the prototype code and fill the buffer with fresh code.

The same listening applies to other people's code. Rather than slogging through it mechanically, jot down the things done in a way that seems strange and look for patterns; if you can see what drove the authors to write it that way, understanding the code becomes much easier, and you can consciously apply the patterns they applied tacitly.
