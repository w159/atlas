# Provide Options, Don't Make Lame Excuses

**In brief:** When something goes wrong, bring solutions and paths forward instead of blame or lame excuses.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 1, Topic 2 "The Cat Ate My Source Code"
**Also known as:** Don't Make Lame Excuses

## What it is
When you make a mistake or hit a problem, do not blame someone or something else. Do not pin the trouble on a vendor, a programming language, management, or your coworkers. Any of these may genuinely play a role, but it is up to you to provide solutions, not excuses.

Before you approach anyone to explain why something cannot be done, is late, or is broken, stop and listen to yourself. Talk it through with the rubber duck on your monitor, or the cat. Does your excuse sound reasonable, or stupid? Run the coming conversation through your mind. What will the other person say? Will they ask "Have you tried this..." or "Didn't you consider that?" If you can already predict the question, save them the trouble and address it first.

Instead of excuses, provide options. Do not say it cannot be done; explain what can be done to salvage the situation. This is the concrete follow-through on taking responsibility.

## Why it matters
Excuses damage trust and reflect on you the way a bank teller's or mechanic's lame excuse reflects on their company. Offering options shows you are engaged and thinking about the problem, not deflecting it. It moves the conversation from fault to progress and keeps you credible with your boss and team.

## In practice
- Run the conversation through your head first; rehearse the likely objections and have answers ready.
- If code has to be deleted, say so and explain the value of refactoring.
- If you need to prototype to find the best path, propose it.
- Suggest better testing or automation to prevent a recurrence.
- Ask for what you actually need: more resources, more time with users, or time to learn a technique or technology in depth. Do not be afraid to admit you need help.
- Flush out lame excuses before voicing them. Tell the cat first if you must.
