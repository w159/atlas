# The Essence of Agility

**In brief:** Agile is an adjective describing how you work, not a process you install; it is about responding to change by taking small steps and acting on feedback.

**Category:** Mindset
**Source:** Chapter 8, Topic 48 "The Essence of Agility"
**Also known as:** Agile Is How You Do Things

## What it is
Agile is an adjective: it is how you do something. You can be an agile developer or a team that adopts agile practices and responds to change with agility. Agility is your style, not a thing you are. Anyone who sells "Agile-in-a-Box," an off-the-shelf process with more management layers, formal reporting, and fancy job titles, has missed the point.

The book returns to the values of the Manifesto for Agile Software Development: individuals and interactions over processes and tools; working software over comprehensive documentation; customer collaboration over contract negotiation; responding to change over following a plan. There is value in the items on the right, but more value in the items on the left. Three of the four values are about gathering and responding to feedback. The values do not tell you what to do; they tell you what to look for when you decide for yourself what to do.

There can never be an agile process. Whenever someone says "do this and you'll be agile," they are wrong by definition, because agility is about responding to the unknowns you meet after you set out. A running gazelle does not go in a straight line; a gymnast makes hundreds of corrections a second. Every decision is contextual, depending on you, your team, your application, tooling, company, customer, and the outside world, so no fixed static plan survives.

## Why it matters
Because you cannot know in advance what change you will face, a rigid plan will fail; the ability to adjust is what keeps a project on course. This is also why good design matters: the measure of design is how easy the result is to change. Step 3 of the recipe says you must fix what you break, and that fix has to be nearly painless or you will be tempted to leave it (software entropy). Good design makes change easy, and easy change is what lets you adjust at every level without hesitation. That is agility.

## In practice
The book's recipe for working in an agile way, applied recursively at every level:
1. Work out where you are.
2. Make the smallest meaningful step toward where you want to be.
3. Evaluate where you end up, and fix anything you broke.

Repeat until done. It applies at the lowest level (renaming a variable can improve a system's design and reduce coupling) and the highest (a first step can reveal that the best solution does not even involve software). Teams should apply it to their own process; a team that does not continuously experiment with its process is not an agile team.
