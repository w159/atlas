# Pair Programming

**In brief:** Two developers work one problem together, one operating the keyboard and one not, switching typing duties as needed.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 8, Topic 47 "Working Together"
**Also known as:** none

## What it is
Pair programming is one of the practices of eXtreme Programming (XP) that has become popular outside of XP itself. One developer operates the keyboard and the other does not; both work on the problem together and can switch typing duties as needed.

The value comes from the division of attention. The typist must focus on the low-level details of syntax and coding style, which consumes real mental bandwidth, while the second developer is free to consider higher-level issues and scope. Having a second full brain available brings far more mental power to the task.

## Why it matters
Different people bring different backgrounds, experience, problem-solving techniques, and levels of focus. Because humans have limited brain bandwidth, offloading the syntax work to the typist lets the partner think about the bigger picture. The inherent peer pressure of a second person guards against moments of weakness and bad habits (naming variables foo and such); you are less likely to take an embarrassing shortcut when someone is watching, which also produces higher-quality software.

## In practice
- If you only program solo, try pairing; give it at least two weeks, a few hours at a time, since it feels strange at first.
- Switch typing duties as needed so both partners stay engaged.
- Decide who is included: just developers, or extended team members like users, testers, and sponsors.
- Manage the human side as much as the technical: criticize the code not the person, listen to other viewpoints, and hold retrospectives.
