# Stone Soup

**In brief:** When you can't get approval to do the whole thing at once, build a small, undeniable piece first and let people rally around the ongoing success.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 1, Topic 4 "Stone Soup and Boiled Frogs"
**Also known as:** Be a Catalyst for Change

## What it is
Three hungry soldiers returning from war reach a village where the residents, short of food after years of war, lock their doors. The soldiers boil a pot of water, drop in three stones, and call it "stone soup." Curious villagers gather. The soup would taste even better with a few carrots, the soldiers muse, and a villager runs off to fetch some. Then some potatoes for body. Then beef, leeks, salt, herbs, each contributed by a different villager. Eventually there is a large pot of real soup, the stones come out, and the whole village shares the first square meal any of them has had in months.

There are two morals. The villagers are tricked, yes, but more importantly the soldiers act as a catalyst, bringing the village together to jointly produce something none of them could have made alone, a synergistic result where everyone wins.

Sometimes you should emulate the soldiers. You may know exactly what needs doing and how, but asking permission to tackle the whole thing gets you delays, blank stares, committees, and budget approvals while everyone guards their own resources. This is sometimes called "start-up fatigue."

## Why it matters
Big up-front requests trigger resistance and turf-guarding, and good ideas die in that friction. People find it far easier to join an ongoing success than to authorize a large unproven plan. Showing a working glimpse of the future turns a hard sell into something others want to be part of, and it produces a genuinely collaborative result rather than something imposed.

## In practice
- Work out what you can reasonably ask for, then develop that small piece well.
- Once you have it, show people and let them marvel.
- Then say, "of course, it would be better if we added..." and treat it as no big deal.
- Sit back and wait for them to start asking you to add the functionality you wanted all along.
- Watch the ethics: catalyzing change that benefits everyone (stone soup) is different from gradual deception that harms (frog soup). See the boiled frog entry.
