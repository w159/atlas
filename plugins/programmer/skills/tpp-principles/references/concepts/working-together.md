# Working Together

**In brief:** Solving problems together while actually coding, not through meetings, memos, and heavyweight documents; do not go into the code alone.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 8, Topic 47 "Working Together"
**Also known as:** Collaborative Coding, Don't Go Into the Code Alone

## What it is
The book's "secret weapon" for impossible projects is working together, and by that it does not mean sharing a massive requirements document, flinging cc'd emails, or enduring endless meetings. It means solving problems together while coding: one person typing while one or more others comment, ponder, and solve problems alongside them. Andy and Dave first met on an impossible project with a ridiculous deadline whose one saving grace was a domain expert sitting across the hall, continuously available.

This spans a spectrum: coding alone, in pairs, or in mobs, in the same office or remote, are all valid ways of working together. Users, testers, and sponsors are part of the team, and the point is to ask questions and have discussions while you are actually coding, not just before.

The chapter also introduces Conway's Law: the communication structures of an organization end up mirrored in the systems it builds. Teams that include users produce software that reflects that involvement; teams that do not reflect that too.

## Why it matters
Collaboration transcends endless meetings, memos, and overstuffed legalistic documentation prized for weight over usefulness. Different people bring different backgrounds, techniques, and levels of focus, and a second brain frees the non-typist to think about higher-level issues while the typist handles syntax. Peer presence discourages embarrassing shortcuts and raises quality. Each style has its own rules and pitfalls, so read up and start small rather than jumping in naively.

## In practice
- Build the code, not your ego; we all have good and bad moments.
- Start small: mob with 4 to 5 people, or start with a few pairs, in short sessions.
- Criticize the code, not the person: "let's look at this block" beats "you're wrong."
- Listen and try to understand others' viewpoints; different is not wrong.
- Hold frequent retrospectives to improve for next time.
- Do not just jump into your toughest production code; try a simple exercise first, and do some reading on each style.
