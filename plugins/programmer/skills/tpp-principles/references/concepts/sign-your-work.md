# Sign Your Work

**In brief:** Put your name to your designs and code the way an artisan signs a craft, so your signature becomes a recognized indicator of quality and you stand behind what you build.

**Category:** Guideline
**Source:** Chapter 9, Topic 53 "Pride and Prejudice"
**Also known as:** pride of ownership, craftsmanship

## What it is
Artisans of an earlier age were proud to sign their work, and the book says you should be too. It is the last tip in the book and a direct consequence of everything before it. Pragmatic Programmers do not shirk responsibility; they accept challenges, make their expertise known, and, when responsible for a design or a piece of code, do a job they can be proud of.

Signing your work is about pride of ownership: "I wrote this, and I stand behind my work." The goal is that your signature comes to be recognized as a mark of quality, so that when people see your name on a piece of code they expect it to be solid, well written, tested, and documented. A really professional job, written by a professional, a Pragmatic Programmer.

The counterweight is anonymity, which especially on large projects becomes a breeding ground for sloppiness, mistakes, sloth, and bad code, because it is too easy to see yourself as just a cog in the wheel producing lame excuses in status reports instead of good code. Code must be owned, though not necessarily by an individual; communal ownership works when paired with practices like pair programming that guard against the dangers of anonymity. Signing also carries a caution: pride can turn into prejudice and territoriality, so the tip only works on a foundation of mutual respect and the Golden Rule, treating others' code as you would have them treat yours.

## Why it matters
Attaching your name to your work aligns your reputation with its quality and pushes you to make it worth signing. Over time a trusted signature lets others rely on your code without re-checking it. Anonymity removes that incentive and lets sloppiness and sloth accumulate unchecked. The risk to manage is that ownership pride can harden into territorial prejudice, which is why signing your work depends on mutual respect to stay healthy.

## In practice
Take clear ownership of your designs and code and be willing to say you stand behind them. Hold your work to a standard, solid, tested, documented, that you would be proud to have your name on. Avoid the cog mindset and the anonymity that excuses bad code. If your team owns code communally, add pair programming or similar practices so shared ownership does not become anonymous ownership. Sign with pride, but pair it with respect for your coworkers' code.
