# Care About Your Craft

**In brief:** There is no point in developing software unless you care about doing it well.

**Category:** Principle
**Source:** From the Preface to the First Edition

## What it is
The first of the two most basic characteristics that all Pragmatic Programmers share, stated as Tip 1. The authors frame programming as a craft: at its simplest, getting a computer to do what you (or your user) want it to do. As a programmer you are part listener, part advisor, part interpreter, and part dictator, capturing elusive requirements and expressing them so a machine can do them justice, all against the ticking of the project clock.

The preface also traces the word "pragmatic" itself: from the Latin pragmaticus, "skilled in business", derived from the Greek word meaning "fit for use".

## Why it matters
The authors present this as basic enough to state as a tip precisely because everything else in the book builds on it. Craftsmanship is not opposed to engineering: they argue that within the overall structure of a project there is always room for individuality and craftsmanship, using the medieval European cathedral builders as the example. The carpenters, stonecutters, carvers, and glass workers were all craftspeople interpreting engineering requirements, and it was their belief in their individual contributions that sustained those projects: "We who cut mere stones must always be envisioning cathedrals."

## In practice
- Do not be wedded to any particular technology; build a broad background so you can choose good solutions in particular situations. There is no best tool, language, or operating system, only systems more appropriate in a particular set of circumstances.
- Treat improvement as a continuous process. The book cites the Eton College gardener (brush off the dew, mow, roll, "do that for 500 years") and the Japanese term kaizen, continuously making many small improvements: work every day to refine the skills you have and add new tools to your repertoire.
- Cultivate the shared characteristics the preface lists: early adopter/fast adapter, inquisitive, critical thinker, realistic, jack of all trades.
