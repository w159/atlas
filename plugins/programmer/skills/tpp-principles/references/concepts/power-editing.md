# Power Editing

**In brief:** Work toward fluency in your editor so editing becomes instinctive and thought flows straight into the buffer.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 3, Topic 18 "Power Editing"
**Also known as:** Achieve Editor Fluency

## What it is
An editor is an extension of your hand more than any other software tool, because text is the basic raw material of programming. The first edition of the book recommended using a single editor for everything (code, documentation, memos, system administration); the authors have since softened that position a little, allowing as many editors as you like while asking that you work toward fluency in each.

Fluency has a concrete challenge list. When editing text: move and select by character, word, line, and paragraph. When editing code: move by syntactic units (matching delimiters, functions, modules), reindent after changes, comment and uncomment blocks with one command, undo and redo, split the window into panels and navigate them, jump to a line number, sort selected lines, search for strings and regular expressions and repeat searches, create temporary multiple cursors from a selection or pattern and edit each in parallel, display compilation errors, and run the project's tests. The final test: can you do all of it without a mouse or trackpad?

## Why it matters
There is a time argument: making editing 4% more efficient at 20 hours a week gains roughly an extra week over a year. But that is not the real benefit. The real gain is that fluency removes the mechanics of editing from conscious thought. The distance between thinking something and having it appear in the buffer drops way down, so your thoughts and your programming flow. The book likens it to driving: an experienced driver controls the car instinctively while a learner thinks about every action.

## In practice
Do not try to memorize every command in a powerful editor; almost no one does. Take a pragmatic approach: learn the commands that make your life easier.

- Watch yourself edit. Every time you do something repetitive, think "there must be a better way," then find it.
- Install each new feature into muscle memory through repetition. Consciously look for chances to use it many times a day; after about a week you use it without thinking.
- Grow your editor. Most powerful editors are a small core augmented by extensions. When you hit a limitation, search for an extension; someone likely published a solution. Dig into the editor's extension language to automate repetitive tasks, often in a line or two. If nothing exists, write a full extension and publish it, because if you needed it others will too.

The challenges push this hard: turn off autorepeat and learn key sequences to move, select, and delete by character, word, line, and block; lose the mouse for a whole week and keep pencil-and-paper notes of the key sequences you learn.
