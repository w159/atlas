# Engineering Daybook

**In brief:** Keep a paper journal of what you did, learned, and thought while working, because the palest ink beats the best memory.

**Category:** Practice
**Source:** Chapter 3, Topic 22 "Engineering Daybooks"
**Also known as:** Daybook, Engineering Journal

## What it is
An engineering daybook is a kind of journal in which you record what you did, things you learned, sketches of ideas, readings from meters, basically anything to do with your work. The practice comes from electronic and mechanical engineers who walked around with a paper notebook, a pen stuffed down the spine, and popped it open to scribble something mid-conversation. When a notebook filled up, they wrote the date range on the spine and shelved it next to the previous ones, with a gentle competition over whose set took the most shelf space.

The book's authors use daybooks to take notes in meetings, jot down what they are working on, record variable values while debugging, leave reminders about where they put things, capture wild ideas, and sometimes just doodle.

## Why it matters
The daybook has three main benefits the book names:

- It is more reliable than memory. When someone asks the name of the company you called last week about the power-supply problem, you flip back a page and give them the name and number.
- It gives you a place to store ideas that are not immediately relevant, so you can keep concentrating on the task at hand knowing the great idea will not be forgotten.
- It acts as a kind of rubber duck. Stopping to write something down switches your brain into a reflective, almost-talking-to-someone gear; you may start a note and suddenly realize the thing you just did is plain wrong.

There is a bonus benefit too: years later you can look back at what you were doing and remember the people, the projects, and the awful clothes and hairstyles.

## In practice
Try keeping an engineering daybook for a month and see if you get any benefit. Use paper, not a file or a wiki: the book insists there is something special about the act of writing compared to typing, and it notes evidence that doodling helps focus and improves cognitive skills. If nothing else, it will make writing your memoir easier when you are rich and famous.
