# Find the Box

**In brief:** Instead of "thinking outside the box," find the box: identify the real constraints and the degrees of freedom, since the box of what is actually allowed is often much larger than you assume.

**Category:** Guideline
**Source:** Chapter 8, Topic 46 "Solving Impossible Puzzles"
**Also known as:** Degrees of Freedom, Don't Think Outside the Box

## What it is
The popular phrase "thinking outside the box" tells you to recognize constraints that might not apply and ignore them, but the book argues the phrase is inaccurate. If the box is the boundary of your real constraints and conditions, the trick is to find that box, which is usually considerably larger than you think.

The key to solving puzzles is both to recognize the constraints placed on you and to recognize the degrees of freedom you actually have, because the solution lives in those degrees of freedom. Puzzles trip people up because they dismiss potential solutions too readily. The book's example is the Four Posts puzzle: connect all of the dots and return to the starting point with just three straight lines, without lifting your pen or retracing your steps.

It is not about thinking inside or outside the box. The real work is finding the box: identifying which constraints are genuine, hard-and-fast limits and which are just preconceived notions you can discard.

## Why it matters
Preconceived, false constraints are what turn solvable problems into "impossible" ones. Challenge each supposed limit and prove whether it is real, and options that were invisible become available. The Trojan horse is the classic example: getting troops into a walled city seemed impossible because "through the front door" was dismissed up front as suicide.

## In practice
- Challenge every preconceived notion; evaluate whether each is a real, hard-and-fast constraint.
- Enumerate all avenues, then explain why each cannot be taken, and test whether you can actually prove it.
- Distinguish absolute constraints (must be honored) from preconceived notions (can be dropped).
- Prioritize by restrictiveness: identify the most restrictive constraints first, then fit the rest inside them.
