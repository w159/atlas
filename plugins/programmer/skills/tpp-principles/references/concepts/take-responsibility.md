# Take Responsibility

**In brief:** Actively commit to doing things right, own your mistakes honestly, and build the team trust that responsibility depends on.

**Category:** Principle
**Source:** Chapter 1, Topic 2 "The Cat Ate My Source Code"
**Also known as:** The Cat Ate My Source Code, Team Trust

## What it is
Taking responsibility is a cornerstone of the pragmatic philosophy. It means taking charge of your career, your learning, your project, and your day-to-day work, and not being afraid to admit ignorance or error. Even on the best projects, things go wrong: deliveries slip and unforeseen technical problems appear. Pragmatic Programmers deal with these honestly and directly, owning up to shortcomings, ignorance, and mistakes.

Responsibility is something you actively agree to. You make a commitment to ensure something is done right, even though you do not directly control every aspect of it. Beyond doing your personal best, you must analyze the situation for risks that are beyond your control. You have the right to decline a responsibility for an impossible situation, one where the risks are too great, or one where the ethical implications are too sketchy. That is a call you make based on your own values and judgment.

Once you accept responsibility for an outcome, expect to be held accountable for it. When you make a mistake or an error in judgment, admit it honestly and offer options rather than excuses.

Responsibility rests on team trust. Your team needs to be able to rely on you, and you need to rely on them. Research shows trust is essential for creativity and collaboration. In a healthy, trust-based environment you can speak your mind and present ideas safely. A breach of trust, like the ninja who left the laser at home because the cat was playing with the red dot, can be hard to repair.

## Why it matters
Trust is the foundation of collaboration; without it, teams cannot create together. Owning your commitments and admitting your errors keeps that trust intact. Dodging blame or inventing excuses erodes it. When a real risk exists, accepting responsibility means planning for it, so that a vendor failure or a disk crash without a backup does not become a story about the cat eating your source code.

## In practice
- Be honest and direct about mistakes; own your shortcomings and ignorance.
- Analyze risks beyond your control before accepting a responsibility, and decline impossible or unethical ones.
- If there was a risk a vendor might not deliver, have a contingency plan. If your storage melts and you have no backup, it is your fault.
- When you say "I don't know," follow it with "but I'll find out."
- Provide solutions, not excuses (see the related concept below).
